# Toastmasters Principles for Technical Presentations

This guide adapts core public speaking principles from Toastmasters for the context of technical and corporate presentations, such as security briefings or program updates.

## I. Strategy & Message

### 1. Know Your Audience

Before writing a single slide, identify your audience. Are you speaking to executives, engineers, or a general audience? Use the **Personas** in `docs/personas/` to guide your tone, content, and level of technical detail.

- **The Boss:** Focus on metrics, risk, and business impact. Keep it brief.
- **The Cynic:** Be prepared with data to back up every claim. Acknowledge potential downsides.
- **The Uninformed:** Avoid jargon. Use analogies and simple explanations.

### 2. The "WIIFM" (What's In It For Me?)

Technical speakers often focus on the "how" (features/tech), but the audience cares about the "why" (benefits).

- **Explicit Statement:** Early in the talk, complete this sentence: "This matters to you because..."
- **Benefit vs. Feature:** A feature is "We enabled MFA." The benefit is "Your account is 99% less likely to be hacked."
- **Respect Their Time:** Answering WIIFM validates why they are spending 15 minutes listening to you.

### 3. The Elevator Pitch (The 30-Second Summary)

You must be able to summarize your entire presentation in 30 seconds. This is crucial for executive pre-briefings or chance encounters.

- **Structure:** Problem -> Solution -> Ask.
- **Example:** "We're seeing an increase in phishing attempts (Problem). My proposal is to roll out new awareness training that has proven to cut click-through rates by 50% (Solution). I need your approval on the $5k training budget to get started next month (Ask)."

## II. Structure & Story

### 4. Structure Your Speech

A clear structure makes your message memorable. Every presentation, no matter how short, should have a distinct opening, body, and conclusion.

- **Opening:** Grab their attention immediately. Don't start with "Hi, my name is...". Use a startling statistic, a relevant story, or a thought-provoking question. Then, state your purpose.
- **Body:** Organize your main points logically. For a 15-minute talk, limit yourself to 3 key points. Support each point with data, examples, or stories.
- **Conclusion:** Summarize your key points and leave the audience with a clear call to action or a memorable closing thought. Never end with "Uh, that's it."

### 5. The Narrative Arc (Advanced Storytelling)

Go beyond a simple structure and create a compelling narrative. A powerful technique is contrasting "What Is" with "What Could Be."

- **What Is:** Start by describing the current state—the problem, the pain point, the challenge. This grounds the audience in a shared reality and creates tension. Make this tangible with a story about a bad experience (real or illustrative) where security was an afterthought.
- **What Could Be:** Paint a picture of the ideal future—the solution, the benefit, the opportunity. This creates desire and a vision for the audience to buy into. Contrast the negative story with a positive one of how your proposal leads to a better, more secure outcome.
- **The Bridge:** Your proposal, project, or idea is the bridge that closes the gap between the unsatisfactory present and the desirable future. People remember stories far more than data points, so this narrative makes your message memorable.

### 6. Data Storytelling (The "So What?")

Technical presentations often fail by dumping raw data without context.

- **Action Titles:** Don't title a slide "Q3 Security Metrics." Title it "Phishing Incidents Dropped 20% in Q3."
- **Declutter:** Remove grid lines, 3D effects, and unnecessary legends ("Chart Junk").
- **Focus:** Use color to highlight the one specific data point you want them to see; gray out the rest.
- **The Takeaway:** Every chart needs a clear conclusion. If there is no "so what," remove the chart.

## III. Visual Design & Components

### 7. What Goes Into a Presentation

A complete presentation package consists of several key components that work together:

- **The Narrative (Script/Outline):** A compelling story arc that connects your opening to your conclusion.
- **Visual Aids (Deck):** Slides, diagrams, or props that reinforce—not repeat—your words. Keep text minimal.
- **Evidence:** Data, metrics, quotes, or anecdotes that validate your claims.
- **Speaker Notes:** Brief cues for the presenter (e.g., "Pause here", "Click to next slide").
- **Handouts/Leave-behinds:** Detailed reference material so the audience can read later, allowing you to keep slides clean.

### 8. The 10-20-30 Rule

A useful framework for keeping technical presentations concise and readable:

- **10 Slides:** The optimal number of slides. Humans struggle to comprehend more than 10 concepts in a meeting.
- **20 Minutes:** You should be able to deliver your presentation in 20 minutes.
- **30 Point Font:** No font smaller than 30 points. This forces you to find the core message and prevents "reading" the slide.

### 9. The "Glance Test" (Visual Efficiency)

Your audience cannot read and listen at the same time.

- **The 3-Second Rule:** If the audience cannot understand the slide's main point in 3 seconds, it is too complex.
- **Visual Hierarchy:** The most important element should be the biggest or boldest.
- **One Concept:** If you are explaining two different complex ideas, split them into two slides. Slides are free.

### 10. Accessibility & Inclusivity

Ensure your presentation is consumable by everyone in your audience.

- **Color Blindness:** Avoid using color alone to convey meaning (e.g., "The red line is bad"). Use shapes or labels directly on lines. Avoid Red/Green combinations.
- **Alt Text:** If sharing the deck, add alt text to images for screen readers.
- **Readability:** Ensure font size and contrast meet WCAG AA standards.
- **Captions:** Enable live captioning for remote presentations to support non-native speakers and those with hearing impairments.

### 11. Minimum Requirements

- [ ] **Presentation**
    - [ ] **Typography:** 30 point font minimum.
    - [ ] **Font Family:** Use sans-serif fonts (e.g., Arial, Verdana) for screen readability.
    - [ ] **Background:** Dark background with white text. It conveys seriousness and makes colors pop.
    - [ ] **Design:** Simple design. Avoid distinct/distracting backgrounds or template decorations.
    - [ ] **Text Density:** Follow the "6x6 Rule" (Max 6 lines of text, Max 6 words per line).
    - [ ] **Focus:** One main idea per slide.
    - [ ] **Visuals:** Use high-quality images; avoid pixelated clip art or distracting transitions.

## IV. Delivery & Presence

### 12. Use Vocal Variety

A monotone voice will put any audience to sleep. Vary your **pace, pitch, and volume** to keep listeners engaged and to emphasize key points.

- **Pace:** Slow down when explaining a critical concept. Speed up slightly to convey excitement.
- **Volume:** Speak more loudly to make a strong point. Speak more softly to draw the audience in.
- **Pause:** A well-timed pause before or after an important statement can be more powerful than words.

### 13. Master Your Body Language

- **Eye Contact:** Connect with your audience by making eye contact with different people in the room.
- **Gestures:** Use natural hand gestures to emphasize your points. Avoid fidgeting or keeping your hands in your pockets.
- **Stance:** Stand tall and confident. Move with purpose, don't pace aimlessly.
- **Stage Usage:** Treat the stage as a visual aid.
    - **Timeline:** Move from the audience's left to right to represent time (Past → Present → Future).
    - **Anchoring:** Assign specific physical spots to specific topics or viewpoints.
    - **Proximity:** Step forward to emphasize a key point; step back to transition.

### 14. The "Ah-Counter" (Filler Words)

Toastmasters specifically tracks filler words because they dilute your message.

- **Common Culprits:** "Um," "Ah," "So," "Like," "You know," "Basically."
- **The Fix:** Pause. A silence is powerful; a filler word is distracting.
- **Technical Fillers:** Watch out for repetitive phrases like "sort of," "kind of," or overuse of acronyms without definition.

### 15. Managing Speaking Anxiety

Feeling nervous is normal; it means you care. The goal is to manage that energy, not eliminate it.

- **Reframe, Don't Repress:** Channel nervous energy into enthusiasm for your topic.
- **Breathe:** Before you begin, take several slow, deep breaths to calm your nervous system.
- **Connect Early:** Arrive early and chat with a few audience members. It's easier to speak to friendly faces than a room full of strangers.
- **Don't Apologize:** Never start by saying you're nervous. The audience likely won't notice unless you point it out. Your passion for the topic is what matters.

## V. Logistics & Environment

### 16. Remote & Virtual Specifics

Modern technical presentations often happen over video conference.

- **Eye Contact:** Look at the camera lens, not the faces on your screen. This simulates eye contact for the remote audience.
- **Audio First:** A good microphone is more important than a good camera. If audio cuts out, you lose the audience immediately.
- **Screen Sharing:** Share only the specific application window, not your entire desktop, to prevent notification embarrassments.
- **Lighting:** Ensure your face is lit from the front. Avoid strong backlighting (windows behind you).

### 17. Contingency Planning

Technology fails. Be prepared.

- **Offline Copy:** Always have a PDF version of your slides downloaded locally.
- **The "No-Slide" Version:** Be prepared to deliver your update verbally if the projector/screen share fails.
- **Demo Videos:** If doing a live demo, have a backup video recording in case the live environment is down.

### 18. The Feedback Loop

In Toastmasters, evaluation is the heart of improvement.

- **Dry Run:** Practice with a colleague (use the "Herd" persona).
- **Time Yourself:** You must respect the audience's time. If you have 15 minutes, aim for 12 to leave buffer.
- **Solicit Specific Feedback:** Don't just ask "How was it?" Ask "Was the risk section clear?" or "Did I speak too fast?"

## VI. Interaction & Closing

### 19. Handling Q&A

Technical presentations often live or die by the Q&A session.

- **Listen:** Listen to the entire question before answering.
- **Repeat:** Paraphrase the question to ensure understanding and ensure the whole room heard it.
- **Bridge:** If the question is off-topic, answer briefly and bridge back to your main message.
- **The "I Don't Know":** Never bluff. Say, "That's a great detail I don't have right now. I will follow up with you by [Date]."

### 20. Post-Presentation Follow-up

The presentation doesn't end when the Q&A is over.

- **Distribute Materials:** Send a follow-up email within 24 hours with the slide deck (as a PDF) and any promised links or resources.
- **Fulfill Promises:** If you said "I'll get back to you," do it. This builds credibility. Track these as action items.
- **Measure Impact:** Was your "Ask" successful? Track whether the budget was approved, the project was greenlit, or the stakeholders took the desired action.
